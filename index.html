Multiple Job-Holders in Australia

Overview

More Australians than ever are holding multiple jobs. This project visualises national and state-level data to show how multiple job-holding has evolved over time, and how it varies by gender and industry. The goal is to highlight key insights about Australia's changing workforce.

Trend Over Time

The visualization shows the trend of multiple job-holding in Australia from 1994 to 2024, highlighting both the number of multiple job-holders and the rate as a share of total population.The rate remained relatively stable between 1994 and 2019, fluctuating around 5.5-6%, before a large decline in June 2020 caused by the COVID-19 pandemic. Since then, the rate has climbed notably reaching the highest record of all time.

Multiple Job-Holding by Sex

Women consistently hold multiple jobs at higher rates than men throughout the entire 30-year period. Females average around 6.5-7%, while males stay closer to 4.5-5%. Both Sexes have a high increase in 2021-2022 with rates climbing to historic highs by 2024-2025. The rates were relatively stable pre-covid with recent months having more volatility.

Prevalence by Greater Regional Area

The map shows the variation in job-holding rates across regional areas and cities. In June 2025, the multiple job-holding rate was highest in Greater Hobart (capital city) and Tasmania (state) and lowest in Sydney and Queensland. In 2023, the multiple job-holding rate reached the highest it's ever been in regional NT. The rate has since declined for all states throughout 2023-2024. Overall, there isn't much difference between the rates of regional areas and capital cities.

Multiple Job-Holding by Industry

Administrative and support services has the highest rate, closely followed by agriculture, forestry and fishing. This makes sense since admin/support work is generally part-time or temporary work and agriculture work is usually seasonal. Overall the range is pretty tight (5-9%), no industry is dramatically different from others.
